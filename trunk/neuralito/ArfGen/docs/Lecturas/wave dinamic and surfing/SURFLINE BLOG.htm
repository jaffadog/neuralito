Strong storm equals surf, but slams sailboat race to Bermuda
Posted on 06/22/09

Any news on the sailboat race heading to Bermuda?
Interesting situation going on. As surfers, we often don't relate to what happens in a storm far out to sea that produces surf along our shores. Sailors often end up in the middle of these storms and give us the opportunity to experience what really happens in these storms out to sea. We currently have a developing storm off the East coast that promises to bring surf to many areas. Surfline's east coast forecasters will have the complete details on the surf forecast.

There's also a sailboat race from Massachusetts to Bermuda that began on Friday. They ran into this same developing storm yesterday and last night with gale force winds out of the northwest, which wasn't too bad because they were going with the wind and seas. One of the relatives of a small boat emailed last night pleading for help with a special weather forecast that we emailed back. We suggested if it's a very small boat like they indicated, that they might want to turn back because it appeared the storm was going to get a lot worse before it gets better.

There's some really cool plotting and tracking going on with the race at iBoat Track and the boats have tracking devices and report on the wind conditions. Some boats have already turned back due to the developing storm.




The fleet is currently in 15-20 knot WSW winds right now which is a pleasant close reach to Bermuda. But the problem is that the 20-35 knot northwesterly winds and 12 foot seas they experienced yesterday and last night were nothing, compared to the stronger 30-50 knot south winds and 15-20 foot+ seas they are probably going to encounter in the last 200 miles before Bermuda, beginning tonight and over the next couple of days. Those south winds and seas will be on the nose and they will need to fight through it before arriving at Bermuda.

We can see that band of strong SSW winds moving up with more tropical moisture in the Quikscat image below that is moving north right into the sailing track of the fleet. As this tropical moisture merges into the storm it will become more super charged with some extra tropical features like much stronger wind speeds and much higher seas.







We can follow this progress over the next couple of days and it is very likely to get very, very difficult for the fleet as they approach Bermuda. Needless to say the wind models are grossly under estimating the wind speeds in this storm, as they often do in semi extra tropical situations with all that tropical moisture being pulled up over the Gulf Stream.

Hopefully everyone in the fleet will stay safe and pull through OK. Extensive marine wind and weather models are available on Buoyweather.com.

Sean...

How do You Define 'Set' Waves?
Posted on 06/19/09

Hello,

I know what 'set waves' are. When I read articles I see the term 'set waves' used, but what are you comparing them to? For example: Surf is inconsistent but the set waves are 'shoulder' high. Set waves in comparison to what other waves? Can anyone shed a little light on this subject?

Thank you,
Jim S.


Hi Jim,

Set waves are those waves that come in larger than the overall significant wave height (Hs). Significant wave height is the largest 1/3 of waves and is the wave height a trained observer would report. However there are statistically going to be larger waves than Hs. The highest 10% of waves statistically are 1.27 times Hs and the highest 1% of waves are 1.67 times Hs - these are the set waves that we refer to. 1 in 1000 waves (H max) can be nearly two times the size of Hs! So with an Hs of roughly 3 feet, we can expect set waves around 3.8 feet (and perhaps larger) which puts set waves in the chest/shoulder high range.

Hope this helps and let us know if you have any further questions.

Best,
Mike Watson
Surfline East Coast Manager/Forecaster

Great Circle Lines and a Big Swell for Indo
Posted on 06/16/09

Hi Kevin,

I was wondering if you could give me some feedback on what is happening in the southern Indian at the moment? Your models are claiming just under 12ft. at the moment, delivery to the Mentawais around late Monday/Tuesday. I've been keeping my eye on the FNMOC isobaric maps, and for the life of me I just can't see where the swell is coming from? I understand the storm is large and deep, but to my eyes the eye of the storm is way south. With a blocking high in the mid-Indian, I'm seeing mostly west to east fetches, not much in the north to south until Monday/Tuesday when it's just off Australia. But that would put more of a 185 S swell up in our neck of the woods around Friday? I'm not seeing what you guys are predicting. I hope I'm wrong, and it won't be hard to tell, but if I am, can you tell me where I'm going wrong?

Thanks in advance for any help,
Cheers,
Christie
Hi Christie-

Keep in mind that when you're viewing a sea surface pressure chart (the FNMOC charts with isobars on them you mentioned), you're looking at a flat map, although our earth is spherical. As such, what looks like a direct west to east fetch on a flat map of the Indian Ocean can actually line up along the great circle lines (the shortest course between two points on the surface of a sphere) toward Indonesia and the Mentawais pretty well. Check out the below image, taken from our Surfline Charts, that shows how the wind in this storm actually lined up along the aforementioned great circle lines toward Bali...different from the Ments, I know, but still gives you a pretty good feel for how this storm and resulting swell were pointed toward you. Ideally, you want to see the isobars line up parallel to the great circle lines for as long as possible; the greater the distance they run parallel to the great circle lines, the longer the fetch of the storm. Also, just for reference, the concentric half rings you see intersecting the great circle lines represent how far a swell with period of 17 seconds can travel in one day (~600 miles).

Now, that being said, this storm didn't line up ideally for Indonesia, as it did have slightly more of a west to east fetch, versus a southwest to northeast fetch. However, with storms of this size, strength and duration, significant swell is pushed out to exposed breaks throughout the East Indian Ocean basin. The swell models came down a bit from the time you wrote your initial email, but in the end it did look like an 8-9', long period south/southwest swell for the Mentawaiis. I'd actually love to get a recap/report of swell and conditions you've seen there in the past couple days, if you have a chance.


Kevin Wallis
Surfline Forecaster

Why are the East Coast Buoys Showing Long Period Swell but Nothing at the Beach?
Posted on 06/11/09

Forecasters,

Why have the NDBC buoys been showing long period swell in the water but nothing rideable is showing at my local break? Yesterday at 11:50 AM the Long Island buoy 44025 was showing 2.6' @ 15 seconds - shouldn't there be some surf?

Thanks,
Northeast Surfer
Very good question NE surfer.

First we must know the data we are looking at. The reading you mention at buoy 44025 on the NDBC site is an overall significant wave height reading. This combines all of the different swells into one reading which can be local windswell/chop along with any other swells in the ocean at that point in time. It is highly important to remember this - at any given point in the ocean, there can theoretically be an infinite number of swells. This is why it is vitally important to break down each individual swell component - breaking the swell down gives the true picture as to what is occurring in the ocean. You can scroll down and see a little more detail in the data but this still is combining swells and again does not break it down into the complete picture. The buoy instruments do provide much more detail and do break down the swell into each individual component. However you can only find this in graphical format by using our LOLA Buoys . Clicking the link will bring you to the LOLA Buoy breakdown of each individual swell at 44025 as shown in the image below



The different colored lines represent each separate swell; at the bottom are the color coded swell periods - just match the colors to the line. We can see that the long period energy from a storm two weeks ao in the Southern Hemisphere is very small - 1' or less. But we can also see a 2' @ 8 second more local SE windswell. Combined these give the coarse readings that you see on the NDBC page.

So there is more to the picture than just what you see at the NDBC site. Simply put, to get the true picture, it is imperative to break down the ocean state into each individual component!

Best,

Shadowed Southwest Swells for Southern California
Posted on 06/10/09

Hi Guys. I've noticed in the Southern California written forecasts that you mention 'shadowed' swells. What are these exactly? Thanks.

-Slim
Hey Slim-

Great question! Certain storms in the Southern Hemisphere are partially blocked, or shadowed, by Tahiti and the other islands of French Polynesia on their way to Southern California. Storms located just to the southeast of New Zealand are the most susceptible to island shadowing (see below screen shots), coming from a direction of roughly 210-220 degrees. Of course, the degree to which a swell is shadowed by French Polynesia is a function of how big the storm is and how long it remains in the Southern California swell window while moving from west to east. Often times we see very broad storms develop in the South Pacific; while the swells from these storms may initially be coming from a partially to heavily shadowed direction (roughly 210-220 degrees), as the storms move eastward and generate a more 'southerly' swell (ie., anywhere from 180-200 degrees), the less it will have to deal with the blocking/shadowing effects of Tahiti and her neighboring islands.








Ok, now that we know what a shadowed swell is, what does it mean for us in terms of surf? As my boss Sean Collins explained to me when I started at Surfline nearly a decade ago, there is a 'cheese grater' effect when swells have to pass through numerous and widespread islands. That is, a significant chunk of size and consistency can and often will be lost from the original swell. The below image is something that our forecast team uses in house every day, displaying what our LOLA swell model is predicting to see, swell wise, in a particular region (the red line on the right side of the page) and comparing that to what the Jason Satellite is actually measuring (the green line on the right side of the page). In this case, the shadowing effect of French Polynesia is quite evident, as LOLA was predicting much larger swell to the north of Tahiti and the Tuamtous (obviously not drawn to scale in this image) than what was actually recorded. Fortunately for us, LOLA actually assimilates this information, so with the next model run the resulting info you see on the long range swell tracking maps, 7-day dashboard and other LOLA products, the swell will be knocked down a notch or two. (FYI, this process is unique to the LOLA swell model...you won't any other models that incorporate this type of real time data and make adjustments on the fly).




The swell has now moved through the shadow of French Polynesia and come out the other side at least partially intact. It's now smooth sailing and good surf for all of Southern California once it reaches our shores a few days later, right? Well, not quite. Now we have to deal with another set of pesky land masses and their shadowing effects, the Channel Islands, which can keep some of the traditional summertime Southern Hemi spots near flat, while just down the coast it's pumping. North OC (especially north of Huntington) and Malibu, not so good on these swells, but Venice pumping (albeit walled)? Yep, you got it. Have a look at the below image for hot and cold spots throughout SoCal on a longer period 210-215 degree swell and also be sure to keep an eye on the SoCal Nearshore Swell Model on any upcoming swells. Oh and certain spots in Northern California love these southwesterly swells too, but we'll save that for another blog.




Enjoy!

Kevin Wallis
Surfline Forecaster

LOLA Forecast Dashboard: Region or Break Specific?
Posted on 06/09/09

I have a question about the LOLA Forcast Dashboard. Ive been a user for a long time and I consistently try to match the timing of a peaking swell with when I paddle out. However, I will often notice that what I expect doesnt actually happen. I paddle out at 2PM expecting a 17s 2.5 foot swell and dont see anything close. Ive looked at swell windows and I dont think that is the issue. I guess my real question is what do the times on the Lola Forecast Dashboard represent? Are those the times the swell is hitting the chosen break, do they represent the time it passes certain buoys, or is it something else all together? Along the same line, is the LFD break specific or region specific?
Hey Lloyd-

The times on the dashboard represent the actual time that a swell is forecast to build, peak, and fade in a particular region according, to our LOLA swell model. The dashboard is specific to a region; for example, the forecast 'data point' for South Orange County is located offshore of Dana Point. You can actually see where each dashboard data point is located by looking Google map with the 'LOLA Global Swell Tracker' tab, just below the forecast dashboard. Just zoom in to a region and scroll over the icon of the little bar graph, as seen below.



Because the dashboard is region specific, you may see discrepancies between what the dashboard is showing for surf heights and what you're seeing at a particular break, depending on the direction and period of the swell. This can be especially true in the case of longer period swells, where the surf may be focusing heavily in one spot and being 'stolen' away from another. In this case, I'd highly recommend also utilizing the LOLA Nearshore Swell Model, which will show hot spots in each region. It's great to use the dashboard as an indicator in the timing and general size of a swell for a region, and then to really nail down whcih spot will be best check those nearshore models or even the LOLA Surf Spot Forecasts, which are specific to a certain spot (ie., Lower Trestles, San-O, etc).

Hope that answers your question. Below are a couple other links that may be helpful-

Ok here it comes (ha ha!) - I also want to add that as forecasters we do more than just assess/interpret the info.

One important and often overlooked part of forecasting is not just model interpretation. Every day when producing a forecast we need to ask several questions - what if the model contains bad data? If the model does contain bad data, where is this occurring? Is the numerical weather model, which drives the wave model, performing poorly? Is the wave model performing poorly? How will this affect the model output? What weaknesses in either the weather or wave model (due to assumptions in the model etc...) may result in bad output?

We are highly trained to assess these questions and then adjust our written forecasts as needed. If we simply wrote down what the model says then we are 'modelcasting' and not 'forecasting' - two extremely large differences and this is what sets us apart from our competitors.

A quick example - the above scenarios of poor model performance can occur with compact low pressure systems and/or distant lows (open ocean lows and tropical systems). For winter lows - we first must forecast the exact timing, track and strength of a low pressure system several days before it impacts our region. We need to assess how the weather and wave models are performing in all of these aspects and then adjust the written forecast as needed. To further add to this - the catalyst for these winter storms takes about 4 days on average to travel from the west coast to the east coast - not very much lead time, but our forecasting techniques are second to none. By assessing the state of the atmosphere and using our knowledge of how the atmosphere is to behave, we can then assess when/where the models are not performing well with these winter storms and thus arrive at a better surf forecast. It is a tedious process, but simply put, we are second to none when it comes to this.

On top of that, LOLA is designed to take wave modeling a step further. We apply data assimilation techniques to adjust for areas of the ocean where data is sparse. We use buoys, observed winds, and satellite overlays of the sea surface height to help arrive at the more accurate solution. Below is a sample image of what is happening behind the scenes as we assimilate real time data for any potential corrections.



Further, our model separates the swell spectrum at every single model point on the globe while the free stuff only gives the basic overall swell height for these points, regardless if there are several different swell heights, periods, and directions for that specific point. This gives a much more accurate depiction of the sea state than the free stuff. The LOLA Nearshore models distill this down further by calculating the surf at your beach after the swell undergoes various processes (such as refraction) due to the underwater bathymetry.

You also have access to 100's of cams with several using high definition technology with many more HD cams to come. Plus you have access to our LOLA Buoys which break down the swell into each of the individual swell height, period, and direction (where available) components. This extremely valuable info is unavailable anywhere else!


Membership also gives access to our LOLA Global Swell Tracker which can give you a custom LOLA forecast at any point on the earth, the LOLA Chart Archives , LOLA Classic Archives , custom surf alerts and any of our expert written forecasts that you may need when traveling to another part of the globe.
