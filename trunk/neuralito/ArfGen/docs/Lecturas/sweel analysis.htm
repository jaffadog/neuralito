Can a weak Indian Ocean low create swell for California?


The origins of significant groundswells are always a source of fascination to surfers and forecasters alike.

In this case we see an inconspicuous mid latitude low forming across the sub-tropical INDIAN Ocean culminating in a heavy Southern Ocean swell event spawning across the entire south-Pacific Basin, delivering swell to locations as diverse as Victoria, Tahiti and California over the coming weeks.

The low pressure originates in the central Indian Ocean around 30ºS, 82ºE on Monday 22nd, tracking rapidly southeast underneath The Bight on Wednesday 24th (Fig. 1). Initially two swells are generated off this storm on the Wednesday; a westerly and a southerly windswell(s). The two swells fill in across the southwest Australian coast on Thursday 25th adding some lump and size to the surf.


Photo: bom.gov.au

This relatively small (warm) low collides with a cold air mass moving westward beneath the continent in the form of a polar low traversing the 65ºS parallel (Fig. 2). They merge and form a supercharged extra-tropical 940 hPa storm beneath Tasmania on Thursday night, intensifying further into the 930’s into Friday morning.

As the storm moves southeast below Tasmania on Thursday and Friday a small pulse of southwest swell is generated off the back of the system. The resulting swell fills in across the Victorian coastline on Friday 26th, leading to a short-lived increase in wave height through the day (Fig. 3).

Meanwhile a high pressure system, tracking slowly across southeast Australia into the Tasman, sets up a subsequent blocking pattern for the east Australian coast, steering the powerful frontal system away to the southeast (Fig. 2).


Photo: bom.gov.au

A resulting storm force (45 – 55 knots) west to north-westerly fetch extends beneath the Tasman Sea on Thursday and Friday, generating a large Open Ocean groundswell crossing the Pacific Ocean over the coming week (Figs. 2 & 3).

Refracted energy generated off the system will produce some large surf in NZ over the weekend; continuing on to produce sizeable surf at Teahupoo, Tahiti early next week.

As the low tracks east across the South Pacific Ocean the fetch is quite zonal (westerly). Due to the high intensity of the low a significant amount of spread in swell is generated off the system, with long period groundswell making landfall in Baja California around the 3rd – 4th October, 2008.

The resulting energy that arrives on the west coast of America and South America has strong initial wave periods of over 20 s, but does not produce spectacular sized surf (maximum wave heights projected in the 2 – 4ft range), due to the vast distances over which the swell must travel.

Whilst a person sitting on a beach in California may not see anything exceptional about the small lines of surf arriving across the coast, it is the origins of the event which are of considerable interest. As forecasters we find it fascinating that a weak low pressure system formed in the Indian Ocean (off Western Australia) one week can have an effect on a Californian surfer’s home break almost two weeks later.

By Ben Macartney & Tom Murray
